## A City Frozen in Stone

Hampi was once the capital of the Vijayanagara Empire, one of the greatest and wealthiest empires in medieval India. At its peak in the 15th and 16th centuries, the city was among the largest in the world, home to half a million people and a thriving centre of trade, art, and religion. Today, its ruins spread across 4,100 hectares of rocky terrain along the Tungabhadra River in northern Karnataka, earning it a coveted spot on UNESCO's World Heritage list.

The landscape itself feels otherworldly. Massive granite boulders — some balanced precariously atop one another — frame the ruins of temples, market streets, royal enclosures, and bathing ghats. Walking through Hampi is like turning the pages of a stone manuscript: each structure tells a story of a civilisation that collapsed almost overnight in 1565, when the allied Deccan Sultanates defeated the empire at the Battle of Talikota.

## The Temples of Hampi

The **Virupaksha Temple**, dedicated to Lord Shiva, is the spiritual heart of Hampi. Unlike the surrounding ruins, it has been in continuous use since the 7th century CE and remains an active place of worship. Its towering gopuram (gateway tower) rises 50 metres and is visible from much of the site.

The **Vittala Temple** is considered the pinnacle of Vijayanagara architecture. Its stone chariot — a replica of a temple chariot with wheels that once rotated — is one of the most photographed monuments in India. The temple's musical pillars, which emit tonal sounds when struck, are a marvel of ancient craftsmanship that continues to puzzle engineers today.

## The Royal Enclosure

The Royal Enclosure contains the Mahanavami Dibba, a stepped platform from which the kings watched festivals and military processions. Nearby, the Lotus Mahal — an elegant two-storeyed pavilion blending Hindu and Islamic architectural styles — served as a pleasure palace for the royal women. The Elephant Stables, a row of eleven domed chambers, once housed the state elephants and stands as a testament to the empire's military grandeur.

## Visiting Hampi

Hampi is best visited between October and February, when the weather is cool and the light is golden at sunrise and sunset. The ruins are split into two main areas: the Sacred Centre (around the Virupaksha Temple) and the Royal Centre (the Mahanavami Dibba and Lotus Mahal). A bicycle or moped is the most practical way to explore the vast site. The nearby village of Hampi Bazaar offers accommodation ranging from basic guesthouses to heritage homestays.

Whether you are a history enthusiast, a photography lover, or simply someone seeking a place unlike anywhere else on earth, Hampi delivers in full measure.
