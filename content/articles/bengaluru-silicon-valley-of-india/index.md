## The Garden City Grows Up

Bengaluru was long celebrated as the "Garden City" — a pleasant, temperate plateau town whose British-era cantonment left behind leafy boulevards, public parks, and a culture of leisure. That identity has not disappeared, but it has been overtaken by a newer, louder one: the technology capital of India, or as the world knows it, Silicon Valley of the East.

The city hosts the Indian headquarters of nearly every major global technology company — Infosys, Wipro, and HAL were built here; Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Flipkart chose it for their largest Indian operations. The Electronic City and Whitefield corridors have become self-contained tech townships, with campuses that rival American tech parks in scale and ambition.

## A Startup Ecosystem Like No Other

Beyond the established giants, Bengaluru's startup scene is arguably its most dynamic feature. The city has produced more unicorns — startups valued at over a billion dollars — than any other Indian city. Firms like Swiggy, Zepto, Razorpay, and Byju's were founded and scaled here, drawing talent from the Indian Institutes of Technology and a steady flow of ambitious graduates who prefer Bengaluru's cosmopolitan energy to quieter alternatives.

Koramangala and Indiranagar are the twin epicentres of startup culture: co-working spaces occupy old bungalows, pitch events happen on weeknights, and the boundary between work and social life dissolves pleasantly over cups of filter coffee.

## The City Beneath the Code

Away from the laptops, Bengaluru rewards the curious traveller. Lalbagh Botanical Garden, established in 1760 under Hyder Ali, shelters a 18th-century glasshouse and trees older than the nation itself. The Bengaluru Palace, built in the Tudor Gothic style in 1878, sits incongruously amid the urban sprawl. The bustling lanes of Chickpet and KR Market offer spices, silks, and jasmine garlands by the kilogram.

The food scene reflects the city's layered identity: traditional Karnataka meals on banana leaves at the ISKCON temple, Tibetan momos in Koramangala, craft breweries in Indiranagar, and the legendary masala dosa at MTR, which has been serving breakfast since 1924.

## Getting There and Around

Bengaluru's Kempegowda International Airport connects to every major Indian city and dozens of international destinations. The Namma Metro network has expanded significantly and now covers most of the city's key corridors, though traffic congestion remains a fact of life. For exploring the old city quarters, auto-rickshaws and two-wheelers are still the most nimble options.
